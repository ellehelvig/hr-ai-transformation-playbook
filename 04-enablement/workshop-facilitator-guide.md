# Workshop facilitator guide

Operational guidance for anyone facilitating the [HR AI literacy curriculum](hr-ai-literacy-curriculum.md) or a standalone AI enablement session. This is the "how to run the room" companion to the curriculum's content — use both together.

---

## Before the session

**Two weeks out:**
- Confirm audience size and mix (all-experience-level sessions need different pacing than a session for one function)
- Send pre-reading (curriculum specifies what to assign per module)
- Book a room or platform that supports the hands-on lab in Module 2 — participants need to actually use AI tools, not just watch a demo

**One week out:**
- Test every prompt in the library you plan to demo, on the actual model/tool your org uses. A prompt that worked when written can drift as models update — verify, don't assume.
- Prepare 2-3 real (or realistically adapted) examples from your own organization. Generic examples land flatter than ones that feel real to the room.
- Identify one AI skeptic to invite deliberately. The best sessions have range in the room, not a room of people who already agree.

**Day of:**
- Arrive early enough to test the tech (screen share, tool access, wifi for the lab)
- Have a backup plan for the hands-on lab if a tool is down or access is blocked for someone — a pre-recorded walkthrough or paired participation works

## Facilitation notes by module

**Module 1 (How AI works):** This is the module most likely to run long because of tangents into "but is my job safe" discussion. Acknowledge the concern directly and briefly, then redirect: "That's real, and it's exactly what Module 3 on governance and Module 4 on scaling responsibly are for. Let's get the mental model right first." Don't skip the redirect — an unaddressed anxiety in the room will resurface and derail later modules.

**Module 2 (Hands-on lab):** This is the highest-value session. If time is tight anywhere in the series, protect this module and cut elsewhere. Walk the room during the lab — don't just present and wait. Most learning happens when you're standing next to someone whose prompt didn't work, asking "what did you actually want it to do?"

**Module 3 (Governance):** Generates the most pushback, usually from two directions: people who think the rules are overkill, and people who think no amount of governance makes this safe. Both reactions are useful signal, not a problem to shut down. Budget real discussion time — this module should not be delivered as a monologue.

**Module 4 (Scaling):** Energy tends to dip here because it's less hands-on than Module 2. Counter this by making the small-group exercise genuinely about their real top-5 ideas, not a hypothetical. The gallery walk at the end works best if you require every group to give one piece of specific feedback to another group, not just admire the work.

## Handling common resistance patterns

| What you'll hear | What's usually underneath it | How to respond |
|---|---|---|
| "This is just going to replace us" | Genuine job security fear | Don't argue it away. Acknowledge it's a real question, point to the human-in-the-loop design principle, and be honest about what is and isn't true for your org's roadmap |
| "AI gets things wrong, why would I trust it" | Bad early experience or unfamiliarity | Agree — that's exactly why verification habits (Module 2) and governance (Module 3) exist. Don't oversell reliability |
| "We don't have time to learn a new tool" | Workload, not resistance to AI specifically | Reframe: the goal is time back, not one more thing. Use a concrete before/after example from the prompt library |
| Silence / disengagement | Could be skepticism, could be past change fatigue | Ask directly in the room: "What's making this feel like it's not worth engaging with?" Don't fill silence by talking more |
| Over-enthusiasm, wants to automate everything immediately | Missing the governance and scope boundaries | Useful energy to redirect, not shut down. Point them to Module 3 and the risk assessment template before they build anything |

## Format adaptations

**Virtual delivery:** Breakout rooms for the Module 4 small-group exercise need a facilitator or co-facilitator per room, not just a timer. The Module 2 lab works virtually but takes longer — build in 15 extra minutes for people to unmute, share screens, and troubleshoot access.

**Self-paced adaptation:** If converting to self-paced, Module 2's lab and Module 3's scenario discussions lose the most value — they depend on live interaction. If you must go self-paced, replace group discussion with a written reflection prompt and a scheduled office hour for questions, rather than dropping the interactive element entirely.

**Single-function audience (e.g., just Talent Acquisition):** Swap in examples specific to that function throughout, especially in Module 2's demo and Module 3's scenario cards. A recruiting team sitting through generic HRBP examples will disengage faster than one working through their own workflows.

## After the session

- Send the prompt library and governance docs as reference material, not just slides
- Collect one thing that worked and one thing that didn't from each cohort — feed it back into this guide
- Track who completed the hands-on lab vs. who just attended — completion of Module 2 correlates most strongly with actual tool adoption afterward
